\section{Neighbourhood pgen (generation degree)}\label{sec:degree}

Point $P_{\text{gen}}$ controls how likely a single CDR3 is to be generated, but chance
homology depends on how many likely \emph{neighbours} a sequence has. Following
mirpy~\cite{mirpy}, we quantify this with the \emph{1-mm neighbourhood pgen}---the
generation probability of all CDR3s within Hamming distance $1$, computed exactly with
OLGA's wildcard identity (\texttt{compute\_hamming\_dist\_1\_pgen}; terminal anchor
positions skipped). Figure~\ref{fig:pgen1mm} shows the per-chain distributions.

\paragraph{The pgen-matched control also matches degree.} immrep25 and its
point-$P_{\text{gen}}$-matched OLGA control have essentially identical neighbourhood-pgen
distributions (median $\Delta\log_{10}\approx 0.01$ on TCR$\beta$, $0.03$ on TCR$\alpha$;
orange and red curves overlap). Matching the point pgen therefore already matches the
generation degree, so the control is valid for homology matchability and immrep25's
residual homology is \emph{not} a neighbourhood-density artefact---consistent with its
being publicity (\S\ref{sec:publicity}), which is removed by chain, not by degree.

\paragraph{Why real data does not ``show more degree'' here.} The homology S/N is
normalised against a random re-partition of each cohort, so it measures epitope
\emph{specificity}, not absolute degree; any cohort with random group labels gives
S/N${=}1$ irrespective of how dense its neighbourhoods are. Absolute degree does differ,
but in the opposite direction to naive expectation: the real AIRR repertoire---sampled
uniformly over \emph{unique} clonotypes---sits at \emph{lower} neighbourhood pgen than
pgen-weighted OLGA generation (median $\sim\!0.8$ dex lower on TCR$\alpha$). Repertoire
diversity is dominated by low-pgen singletons; the high-degree public clones are a small
minority by unique count and would dominate only under abundance weighting.
